Everything around us has been constantly changing at a very fast rate. The world, as we once knew it, isn’t the same. As such, it has become very important to focus on ways to get the most out of the available resources in the right way, without compromising on the essential needs for the future. This has been made possible by creating ‘Sustainable Development’ goals - an approach that the United Nations adopted in the year 2015, to focus on reducing and resolving environmental, economic and social issues.

For the 17 different sustainability goals put forward by the United Nations, and these goals intended to be achieved by 2030, it would be quite fascinating to observe which countries or regions are leading today and which of those are lagging behind; whether the sustainable goals pertaining to different areas of environment, social and economy are related to one another; which goals are the ones that have been achieved by almost all the countries; how the different regions are performing, overall..the list of questions about this dataset can be never-ending!

2. Understanding the Data

Published on the eve of the 2023 Paris summit for a new Global Impact, the dataset on “Sustainability Development Goals”, is available for download on Kaggle and is annually updated.

The core data on sustainable development in the dataset, which comes from the UN Statistics Division’s Open SDG Data Hub, has been provided by the SDG Transformation Center. The SDG data in the data hub is collected annually from regional and national-level organizations of various countries. The collected data is obtained from different types of studies - cohort, case-control, cross-sectional, qualitative, to name a few. It is then compiled and verified globally by custodian agencies of the United Nations(UN). For example, UN custodian agencies like the World Health Organization(WHO) and United Nations Children’s Fund(UNICEF) compile and verify the data related to waste water, drinking water, sanitation, hygiene at the global level.

4.2. Hypothesis Testing

Up till now our data analysis was mostly based on country-wise SDG goal scores. We seek answers in this part, through hypothesis testing, to know whether the progress made on the SDG goals, on average, the same or they differ region-wise. Below, the two-sample t-test and the one-way ANOVA test will be performed. The focus for the two-sample test will be on the scores related to economic growth and for the ANOVA test, the focus is on the scores related to the progress made so far on gender equality and women empowerment by different regions.

4.2.1 ONE-WAY ANOVA TEST

Null hypothesis : There is no difference in the mean scores obtained, for progress made in gender equality and empowerment of women, between the regions East & South Asia, East Europe & Central Asia and MENA.

Alternate hypothesis : The mean scores related to gender equality and women empowerment for the regions East & South Asia, East Europe & Central Asia and MENA are not same.

Let us first filter the necessary data for our test purpose.

#Filter data
sdr_data_hyp<-select(sdrdata,region,goal_5_score)
sdr_data_hyp1 <-filter(sdr_data_hyp,region %in% c("E. Europe & C. Asia","East & South Asia","MENA"))
head(sdr_data_hyp1,4)

Reference Distribution : We have our data now. Before proceeding, it is to be noted that the assumptions of the ANOVA test are same as the general assumptions for any parametric test.

  1. All the observations are independent.
  2. The values of the variable “goal_5_score” follow a normal or near-to normal distribution.
  3. The variation within each region being compared is quite similar for every group.
  4. We set the significance level, denoted by α, to 0.05.

The one-way ANOVA test follows the F-distribution. The probability that we calculate is the probability of observing an F-statistic that is at least as high as the value that we obtain from the study of our data.

Let us take a quick look at the data to see if we are good with our assumptions.

EECA_region<-filter(sdr_data_hyp1,region %in% c("E. Europe & C. Asia"))
qqnorm(EECA_region$goal_5_score)
qqline(EECA_region$goal_5_score) #QQ plot of goal 5 scores for E. Europe & C. Asia

ESA_region<-filter(sdr_data_hyp1,region %in% c("East & South Asia"))
qqnorm(ESA_region$goal_5_score)
qqline(ESA_region$goal_5_score)  #QQ plot of goal 5 scores for East & South Asia

MENA1_region<-filter(sdr_data_hyp1,region %in% c("MENA"))
qqnorm(MENA1_region$goal_5_score)
qqline(MENA1_region$goal_5_score) #QQ plot of goal 5 scores for MENA

v2 <- bartlett.test(goal_5_score ~ region, data = sdr_data_hyp1) #Bartlett-test for variance comparison
v2
## 
##  Bartlett test of homogeneity of variances
## 
## data:  goal_5_score by region
## Bartlett's K-squared = 0.30153, df = 2, p-value = 0.86

Thus, the qqplots show the distributions quite close enough to normal for the three regions. Most of the points fall on the reference line. Through the Bartlett test we observe that the p-value comes out to be greater than 0.05, concluding that there is no evidence to suggest that the variance in Goal_5_score is statistically significantly different for the three regions.

Rejection Criteria : If our p-value(level of marginal significance) obtained after the calculation of F-statistic comes out to be less than or equal to our set significant level of 0.05, we will reject our null hypothesis. Otherwise, we will accept it.

We will now be performing the one-way ANOVA test and interpret the results.

Test Statistic

GE_aov <- aov(goal_5_score~region, data = sdr_data_hyp1) #Performing ANOVA test
summary(GE_aov)
##             Df Sum Sq Mean Sq F value   Pr(>F)    
## region       2   3553  1776.3   12.54 3.26e-05 ***
## Residuals   55   7792   141.7                     
## ---
## Signif. codes:  0 '***' 0.001 '**' 0.01 '*' 0.05 '.' 0.1 ' ' 1

As can be seen from the above output, Df = 2. Df represents the degrees of freedom for our region variable. In this case, there were 3 regions, so this value is: 3-1 = 2.
Df Residuals represent the degrees of freedom for the residuals. In this case, there were 58 observations and 3 regions, so this value is: 58 -3 = 55.
Sum Sq region gives us the sum of squares associated with the variable region. This value is 3553.
Sum Sq Residuals gives us the sum of squares associated with the residuals or “errors.” This value is 7792.
Mean Sq. region gives us the mean sum of squares associated with region. This is calculated as Sum Sq. region / Df. In this case, this is calculated as: 3553 / 2 = 1776.3
Mean Sq. Residuals gives us the mean sum of squares associated with the residuals. This is calculated as Sum Sq. residuals / Df residuals. In this case, this is calculated as: 7792 / 55 = 141.7
F Value is the overall F-statistic of the ANOVA model. This is calculated as Mean Sq. region / Mean sq. Residuals. In this case, it is calculated as: 1776.3 / 141.7 = 12.54
Pr(>F) is the p-value associated with the F-statistic with numerator df=2 and denominator df=55. In this case, the p-value is 3.26e-05, which is an extremely tiny number.

Conclusion : The p-value in our ANOVA table (3.26e-05) is less than 0.05(our set significant level), so we have sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis. Thus, we can say that the mean scores related to the progress made on gender equality and women empowerment is not the same between the three regions.

4.2.2 TWO SAMPLE T-TEST

Null hypothesis : The mean scores for economic growth in both LAC and MENA regions are the same.

Alternate hypothesis : The mean score for economic growth in LAC region differ with the mean score for economic growth in the MENA region.

Let us first filter the data, focusing only on LAC and MENA regions.

#Filter data
sdrdatahyp<-select(sdrdata,region,goal_8_score)
sdr_data_hyp2 <-filter(sdrdatahyp,region %in% c("LAC","MENA"))
head(sdr_data_hyp2)

We now have data of the scores related to the respective economic growth of sample countries belonging to the LAC or MENA region respectively. Simply by looking at the data, it’s hard to draw any solid conclusions. Thus, by using the two-sample t-test, we will try to make decisions in a common, statistically valid manner.

Reference Distribution : The two-sample t-test follows a t-distribution. On performing the t-test, the test statistic shows if the computed statistic is a more extreme value than expected from the t-distribution.

Below are some assumptions we make for our data before proceeding.
1. We assume the data set is a simple random sample from the population.
2. We assume the data are normally distributed.
3. We assume the variances for both regions are equal.
4. We set the significance level, denoted by α, to 0.05.

Let us take a quick look at the data.

LAC_region<-filter(sdr_data_hyp2,region %in% c("LAC"))
qqnorm(LAC_region$goal_8_score)

We can now use the values obtained by performing summary statistics of the data to compute our test statistics.

Rejection Criteria : If our p-value(level of marginal significance) obtained after the calculation of test-statistic comes out to be less than or equal to our set significant level of 0.05, we will reject our null hypothesis. Otherwise, we will accept it.

Let us now start with calculating our test statistic.

First, we find the difference between the two means of the two regions.

Difference_of_group_averages = 72.75 - 67.34

This difference in our samples estimate the difference between the population means for the two regions.

print(2*pt(q=t, df=37, lower.tail=FALSE))

5. Conclusion

5.1. Conclusion

Through this report, the 2023 UN SDG goals were analysed. Exploratory data analysis, principal component analysis and hypothesis testing were performed that helped to understand the patterns and characteristics of the dataset. While Principal component analysis helped to answer that it is possible to understand almost 80% of this dataset by using a fewer number of dimensions; exploratory data analysis and hypothesis testing helped in answering our questions related to the current standings of countries, identifying those at the forefront and those falling behind; as well as how various regions are faring on the whole. In addition, these methods also helped to understand that despite one region making progress similar to another, it doesn’t necessarily imply that they are achieving the same goals.
As the year 2023 comes to conclusion, it is imperative to reflect on the progress and challenges surrounding the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). These 17 goals have served as a beacon of hope and a roadmap for global collaboration to address the world’s most pressing issues. The pursuit of the SDGs requires an unwavering commitment from governments, businesses, communities, and individuals worldwide.

5.2. Limitations

This study has some limitations. The analysis performed is on the overall SDG data of the UN for the year 2023; the entire dataset of SDG since the year it started, should also be analysed for more thorough understanding of the progress being made by the different countries of the world. Another limitation is related to the fact that not all countries of every region provide their progress report to the UN. As such, whatever data is collected by the UN from the countries that report their progress, the analysis is carried out on that basis. Moreover, the SDG dataset will keep changing every year. Hence, the data analysis performed here should not be considered as the final progress report of the countries toward sustainable development.

5.3. Future Scope

Exploratory data analysis and inference of the entire dataset of the UN SDG goal, since the time it started, can be performed as part of future research work. Further, systematic review can also be done to understand the scoring system used to aggregate the indicators related to each of the 17 goals.
